    • Carpio, Morales ask SC: Compel government to explain Parlade red-tagging post


    Written by Zoe Gregorio, Annika Guce, Carina Gutierrez



    In every democratic country, the citizens play a very important role in making the country work or function. Therefore, to be able to properly and responsibly play this very important role, each citizen must keep in mind certain requirements. The “Citizenship in Democracy” consists of three requirements such as; participation, civility and patience. In the article, Lieutenant General Antonio Parlade Jr. posted on his Facebook statements threatening civilians for signing petitions against the anti-terror law.


    “Let’s be watchful of these individuals, groups, and organizations opposing a law that will protect our citizens from terrorists. What’s their agenda?…The Day of Judgment is upon you and the Filipino people, who have suffered enough from the malignant hands of the CPP NPA NDF of which you are part of, sit in judgment,” followed by “Very soon, blood debts will be settled. The long arm of the law will catch up on you, and your supporters.”


    With this you can see how the citizens handled this. The citizens, specifically lawyers, took matters into their own hands and made a 5-page motion asking for clarification on whether what Parlade posted was directly from the government or was it simply just his opinion. This act showed how they followed all three requirements of “Citizenship in Democracy”. The citizens showed the first requirement participation, by participating in making the motion. Making this motion showed both civility and patience, because instead of drilling Parlade on social media accounts they gave him a chance to clarify if what he posted was a form of a threat or not through the High Court.


    Democracy, by definition, is a system of government in which power is vested in the people and exercised by them directly or through freely elected representatives. However, the recent issue about the anti-terror law seems to be against democracy in our country. The article states that Lieutenant General Antonio Parlade Jr. is committing ‘’terrorism’’ by endangering the lives of anti-terror law petitioners. As seen in his social media post: “Very soon, blood debts will be settled. The long arm of the law will catch up on you, and your supporters’’, he clearly threatens the petitioners’ right to seek judicial relief. Moreover, posing a threat like this creates a climate of fear not just to activists and petitioners, but also to average Filipino citizens. As a democratic country, we have the right to safeguard our democracy especially in this kind of situation.


    Aside from the act of filing petitions against the Anti-Terror Law, the last line reflects another aspect of democracy in action. In their 5-page motion, the lawyers said that Calida must be made to confirm whether Parlade’s post was an official communication of the government or an official, and explain the “details regarding the source, circumstances behind, and intent of the post. Calling for Parlade to be accountable for his message on social media is in line with the fruit of democracy which is accountability. A democratic society should not tolerate non-transparency from government officials, especially when it comes to matters like red-tagging. Citizens have the right to know if they will be monitored and punished for expressing their ideologies as well as the right to speak up against this policy. However, citizens cannot properly participate and exercise their freedom to oppose this ruling if they are unaware of the truth behind it.
